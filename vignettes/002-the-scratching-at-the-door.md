# The Scratching at the Door

*As told to B.B. Beauchamp by Sir Willem, Breaker of the Dark Host*

---

Sir Willem, Breaker of the Dark Host, is not a man given to sentiment. So when his voice trembled in the telling of what I shall now relate, I confess my quill faltered more than once.

He received me in the chapel where he keeps his vigil - an old man now, his hair gone to grey, his scarred hands folded before him. But when he spoke of what happened in those catacombs beneath Dunmorrow, his voice became the voice of a child again. A boy of thirteen winters, carrying a torch for his master, descending into a darkness from which he did not expect to return.

---

The rain had not stopped for three days.

It was not the honest rain of summer storms, the kind that announces itself and passes. This was the rain of the northern coast in late autumn: a grey mist that hung in the air and seeped through wool and leather until the cold lived in your bones. The sky offered no sun, no shadows, no sense of time. Only the slow fade from dim grey to darker grey told Willem that evening approached.

The boy Willem walked behind the horses, as was his place. Sir Bram rode at the front, his cloak pulled tight, the lantern-and-star of Saint Aldara barely visible beneath the wet wool. The squire Jorin rode behind him, seventeen and broad-shouldered, close to earning his spurs. The boy carried the unlit torches wrapped in oilcloth, the flint and steel, and a small pack of supplies. His boots squelched in the mud of what the locals generously called a road.

They had been summoned three days prior. A message had reached the Chapel of Saint Aldara in Thornwick - a hamlet near Dunmorrow was troubled. Something had been seen in the old graveyard. Livestock found torn apart, drained of blood, the wounds unlike any animal attack. And then the first body: a gravedigger who had stayed late into the evening, found the next morning in pieces.

A ghoul, the message said. Send help.

Sir Bram had answered, as he always answered. He had brought the squire Jorin, who needed the experience before his final trials. And he had brought the boy Willem, because a torchbearer's place was with his knight, and because (as Sir Bram had said with a hand on the boy's shoulder) there was no better way to learn whether one had the calling than to see the work done.

The boy had been eager then. He was less eager now, with the rain soaking through his hood and the hamlet emerging from the mist.

---

Word traveled faster than horses. By the time they reached the village edge, the sheriff was there to meet them.

He was a heavy man, ale-flushed and soft, with the look of someone who had never had to be brave. His hands would not stay still. When Sir Bram dismounted and approached, the sheriff took a step backward before catching himself.

"You came," he said. "Thank the saints, you came. There's food and beds at the inn, you'll want to rest after—"

"Take me to the graveyard," Sir Bram said.

The sheriff's face went pale, but he nodded.

"Tell me what you know as we walk," Sir Bram said. His voice was calm, unhurried. Willem had heard that voice many times - it was the voice Sir Bram used when others were frightened, a steady current against their panic.

The sheriff spoke quickly, his words tumbling. The graveyard had been undisturbed for generations. But three weeks ago, a sinkhole had opened near the old crypt, the crypt from the old times, from when they buried their dead differently. The gravedigger stayed too late into the evening, finishing a burial. Come morning, they found what was left of him scattered near the hole.

"And the others?" Sir Bram asked.

"Two more since. A farmer's boy who took a shortcut through the graves after dark. A woman gathering herbs at the edge of the wood." The sheriff's voice dropped. "We found... parts of them. Not all."

Sir Bram nodded slowly. "Show me the hole."

---

The crypt was old - older than the wooden church that stood nearby, older perhaps than the hamlet itself. The crypt's masonry was ancient, its stones worn smooth, its carvings eroded beyond recognition. The iron gate that had once sealed the entrance hung open, rusted through.

Beyond the gate, stairs descended into darkness. And at the bottom of those stairs, the floor had given way. A hole, perhaps five feet across, opened into nothing.

Willem stood at the edge and looked down. His torch - lit now, the flame hissing against the damp - illuminated rough stone walls, a drop of perhaps ten feet, and then a passage leading away into blackness.

"How far does it go?" Jorin asked. The squire had moved up beside Willem, peering down.

"We don't know," the sheriff said from behind them. He had not entered the crypt. "No one's gone down. We thought - we hoped - if we just left it alone..."

"You hoped it would leave you alone in return," Sir Bram said. There was no judgment in his voice, only understanding. "That is not how such things work. Evil that finds a home will make itself comfortable. It will grow hungry. It will want more."

He turned to Willem. "Light another torch. Give it to Jorin. You will carry the third. Stay behind us, and keep the flames high. Such creatures hate the light; it will not stop them, but it may slow them."

Willem's hands trembled as he unwrapped a fresh torch and touched it to his own. The pitch caught, the flame rose, and he handed it to Jorin.

Sir Bram drew his sword. The blade caught the torchlight and held it.

"Sheriff," he said, "if we do not return, send word to the Chapel of Saint Aldara. They will know what must be done."

The sheriff nodded, relief and shame warring on his face.

Sir Bram lowered himself into the hole. Jorin followed. Willem went last, dropping into the darkness below, the torch held high above his head.

The passage swallowed them.

---

The catacombs were older than old.

Willem understood this the moment his feet touched the floor below. The walls were not rough-hewn, as he had expected, but smooth - worked stone, fitted together with a precision that spoke of skill and time. Niches lined the passage, each one holding the remnants of what had once been a body. Bones, brown with age. Scraps of cloth that crumbled at a glance. The dry smell of centuries.

"These are not recent," Jorin murmured. "A hundred years? Two hundred?"

"More," Sir Bram said. "These are from before the One God and the Lantern. Before Saint Aldara brought her light to these shores. The people here buried their dead in the old way, beneath the earth, close to whatever gods they worshipped then."

He moved forward, sword raised, his torch casting long shadows. Willem followed, trying to watch everywhere at once. The passage branched ahead - one way left, one way right, both disappearing into darkness beyond the reach of their flames.

Sir Bram paused, listening.

Somewhere ahead, something moved.

---

The passages twisted deeper. Twenty minutes in, they found the nest.

It was a chamber, larger than the passages that led to it, with a ceiling that vanished into shadow. The floor was covered in bones - not the ancient bones of the niches, but fresh ones, cracked and gnawed, scraps of meat still clinging to them. The smell hit Willem like a fist. Rot. Decay. Something sweeter underneath, something worse.

"One ghoul did not do this," Jorin said. His voice had changed. The confidence was still there, but something else had crept in beneath it.

"No," Sir Bram agreed. "It did not."

Willem saw them then.

Eyes. Reflecting the torchlight from the darkness at the edges of the chamber. One pair. Then another. Then more than he could count.

"Back," Sir Bram said. "Slowly. Keep the torches high."

They retreated. Step by step. The eyes followed. Willem's heart hammered against his ribs. The torch shook in his hand. He could hear breathing now - wet, rasping, hungry.

They reached the passage. The eyes remained in the chamber, watching.

"They're not following," Jorin whispered.

"No," Sir Bram said. And then, quieter: "They don't need to. They know these tunnels. They're not chasing us. They're herding us."

---

The ambush came at the third junction.

They moved fast, Sir Bram leading, taking turns without hesitation. At the third junction, the first ghoul came out of the wall.

It emerged from shadow. Humanoid but wrong, limbs too long, fingers ending in claws that scraped against stone. Its face was a ruin, flesh pulled back from teeth that had grown too large for its mouth. Its eyes held nothing like thought. Only hunger.

Jorin shouted, sword in one hand, torch in the other. The ghoul flinched from the flame, hissing - but it did not flee. Others emerged behind it. The passage ahead filled with them.

"The other way!" Sir Bram commanded, turning.

But the other way was no longer clear. More shapes. More eyes. They had been moving through the walls, through passages the torchlight had never reached, and now they closed in from every direction.

Sir Bram moved.

Willem had trained under the knight for two years, but he had never seen Sir Bram fight for his life. The sword was a blur. The first ghoul fell, its head separated from its body. The second took a thrust through the chest and collapsed, clawing at the blade. Sir Bram kicked it free and spun, catching a third across the throat.

"Jorin! The door!"

Willem saw it then - an iron door set into the wall of the passage, ancient and rusted but intact. Jorin threw his shoulder against it. The hinges screamed. The door moved.

But the ghouls were faster.

Three of them hit Jorin at once. He went down beneath them, still holding his torch, still fighting. Willem saw claws tear through leather. Saw the squire's face twist in pain and rage. Heard him shout something: a name, perhaps, or a prayer. And then the ghouls bore him down into the darkness and the shouting stopped, replaced by sounds that Willem would hear in his dreams for the rest of his life.

Sir Bram grabbed Willem by the collar and hurled him through the door.

The knight followed, turning at the threshold, his sword taking the arm off a ghoul that reached for him. Then he slammed the door shut. A rusted bolt remained in its housing. He drove it home.

The scratching began immediately.

---

For a moment - just a moment - Willem thought they were safe.

Then Sir Bram turned from the door, and Willem saw his face change.

Three ghouls had made it through.

Sir Bram killed them.

It was not clean. It was not glorious. The space was too small for proper swordwork, and the creatures fought with the mindless ferocity of the starving. Willem pressed himself against the far wall, torch raised, and watched his master fight.

One ghoul went down with a sword through its eye. The second caught Sir Bram's arm in its teeth and tore before the knight could drive his blade through its skull. The third leapt onto his back, claws raking across his shoulders. Sir Bram roared - the first sound of pain Willem had ever heard from him - and threw himself backward against the wall, crushing the creature. His sword found its heart.

And then there was silence.

Silence, except for the scratching.

---

Sir Bram did not rise immediately.

He lay against the wall where he had fallen, his sword still in his hand, his chest heaving. Blood soaked his surcoat. His arm hung wrong. In the torchlight, Willem could see the wounds - deep gashes across his back and shoulders, the ragged bite on his forearm, something dark and wet spreading across his side where a claw had found its way through his armor.

"Sir Bram?" Willem's voice came out as a whisper.

The knight's eyes opened. They found Willem. They held something the boy had never seen there before.

"The torch," Sir Bram said. "Keep it... high. They hate the light."

Willem raised the torch higher. His arm ached. He did not lower it.

The scratching at the door grew louder. Claws against iron. Claws against stone. The wood of the frame beginning to splinter. How many were out there? A dozen?

Sir Bram tried to rise. He made it to one knee before the strength went out of him. He slumped back against the wall.

"They'll get through," Willem said. He was surprised by how steady his voice sounded. He did not feel steady. He felt like a leaf in a storm. "The door won't hold."

"No," Sir Bram agreed. "It won't."

The knight's eyes closed. His lips moved, but no sound came out at first. Then Willem heard it - words, whispered, in a cadence he recognized from the chapel. A prayer. Sir Bram was praying.

Willem watched the door. The scratching was constant now, a rhythm of hunger and patience. The hinges groaned. How long before it gave way? Minutes? Less?

He thought of Jorin. Of the sounds he had heard. He thought of his mother, far away in Thornwick, who had wept when he left to serve the knight. He thought of all the things he had wanted to do, wanted to see, wanted to become.

The torch flickered in his hand.

Sir Bram's prayer grew quieter. Then stopped.

Willem looked at his master and saw that the knight's eyes had closed. His chest did not rise. His face was pale as parchment, his lips grey. The blood no longer flowed from his wounds. There was none left to flow.

Sir Bram of Thornwick was dead.

This is how it ends, Willem thought. In a room beneath the earth, in the dark, with the dead scratching at the door.

He raised the torch a little higher and waited for the end.

---

The light did not come from his torch.

It came from everywhere.

Willem did not see the angel arrive - one moment the room held only shadow and torchlight, and the next it was filled with a radiance that had no source, a warmth that pressed against his skin like summer sun. He shielded his eyes, but even through his fingers, he could see.

She stood above Sir Bram's fallen form - a figure wrought of light, wings folded at her back, her face terrible and beautiful beyond any mortal measure. She wore armor of gold that did not gleam but glowed, as if the metal itself were flame made solid. In her hand she held a lantern, and within that lantern burned a fire that cast no shadow.

The scratching at the door faltered - but only for a moment.

The angel looked at Willem. Her eyes were not eyes - they were depths, endless, ancient, knowing. He could not hold her gaze. He looked down, trembling.

"Be not afraid," she said.

Her voice was not one voice but many: a chorus speaking in perfect unity, each word resonating in Willem's chest like the deepest bell.

"The Lantern does not abandon those who carry its flame into the darkness."

She knelt beside Sir Bram. A hand of light reached out and touched the knight's chest. Where her fingers met his flesh, light bloomed. Warmth flooded the room. Willem felt it wash over him, through him, driving out the cold that had lived in his bones since they entered this place.

Sir Bram's eyes opened.

Color returned to his face. His arm straightened. The wounds across his back and shoulders closed, leaving only faint scars. He drew a breath, a real breath, deep and strong, and pushed himself upright.

He looked at the angel, and Willem saw something pass between them: understanding, perhaps, or purpose.

His hand found his sword.

Then the angel turned to Willem once more.

"Remember this," she said. "When the darkness comes again, remember that the light answers."

She raised the lantern.

The radiance swelled.

And when Willem could see again, she was gone.

---

Sir Bram rose to his feet, his sword in his hand, his eyes on the door.

The scratching had not stopped. They were still out there, clawing, hungry.

The knight looked down at Willem. His face was calm, the pain gone, only purpose remaining.

"On your feet, torchbearer," he said. "Stay behind me."

Sir Bram turned to the door and drew back the bolt.

The door swung open. The passage beyond was not empty. Eyes reflected the torchlight and the ghouls surged forward.

Sir Bram raised his sword and charged to meet them.

---

*What became of the ghouls beneath Dunmorrow, I shall save for another telling. Sir Bram and his young torchbearer emerged from those catacombs at dawn, bloody but alive, and the work that followed was the work of flame and consecrated earth. Of Jorin, the squire who fell in that terrible ambush, a marker was placed in the graveyard above, though his body was never recovered.*

*But this tale was not about the ending. It was about the beginning.*

*Sir Willem told me, as we sat in that chapel decades later, that he had doubted many things in his youth. His courage. His purpose. Whether the saints truly watched, or whether the prayers were merely words spoken into silence.*

*He never doubted again.*

*For he had seen the Lantern answer. He had felt its warmth in the coldest dark. And when he followed his master through that door, torch held high, he followed not as a torchbearer, but as something more.*

*That night, in a room beneath the earth, a boy held a torch while his master died and rose again. That night, the boy followed a knight through a door into a horde of the hungry dead. That night, Willem's road to becoming the Breaker of the Dark Host began.*
