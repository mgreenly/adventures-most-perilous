# The Collector of Tales

*B.B. Beauchamp - Adventures Most Perilous*

---

The man regarded me across the table with the particular wariness reserved for tax collectors and madmen. I had introduced myself. I had explained my purpose. And yet here we sat, at an impasse.

"So you want me to tell you what happened."

"I do."

"And you're going to write it down."

"Yes."

"And then what?"

"Then I travel to the city. The tale is printed on a broadsheet - a large paper, news and notices and such - and people read it."

He squinted. "People read it."

"They do. In taverns. In markets. Wherever the broadsheets are sold."

"And they pay for this."

"They pay a copper for the broadsheet. My tale is but one part of it, alongside news of ships and grain prices and proclamations from lords."

The man sat back, arms folded. "So the people pay the broadsheet. Does the broadsheet pay you?"

"It does."

"And what do I get?"

"I beg your pardon?"

"You sell my story. The broadsheet sells your story. Everyone's getting paid except me. What do I get?"

I considered the question. It was not the first time I had faced it.

"You get to be heard," I said. "What happened to you - what you survived - right now it lives only in your memory and the memories of those few who witnessed it. Memories fade. People die. In ten years, twenty, who will know? Who will remember what you did?"

He said nothing.

"But a story that is written down, printed, passed from hand to hand - that endures. A clerk in a city two hundred miles from here will read of your courage over his morning bread. A child not yet born will hear your tale read aloud and think: *there was someone brave, once, in a place I have never seen.*"

"You're saying I'd be famous."

"I'm saying you'd be *remembered*. There is a difference." I leaned forward. "I cannot offer you coin. I can offer you this: your story, told true - or true enough - to anyone who cares to listen. Long after both of us are dust."

The man was quiet for a long moment. He looked at my ink, my paper, my waiting hands.

"True enough," he repeated. "What does that mean, *true enough*?"

"It means I may... improve certain details. For the reading."

"Improve."

"The heart of it stays. The meaning. But if your horse was brown, perhaps in my telling it becomes black as midnight. If you fought three men, perhaps it becomes five. The spirit of the thing, you understand. The *truth* of it, if not the facts."

He almost smiled. "So you're a liar."

"I am a storyteller. The distinction is subtle but, I maintain, important."

Another silence. Then the man unfolded his arms and reached for his drink.

"Alright," he said. "But the horse really was black. And it was five men."

I uncapped my ink.

"Then we are already off to an excellent start."

---

*Thus does every tale begin - not with the event itself, but with the telling of it. The convincing. The moment when someone decides their story deserves to be heard, and B.B. Beauchamp is granted the privilege of hearing it.*
